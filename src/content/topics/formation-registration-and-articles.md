## Introduction

This unit treats formation as both private ordering and public administration. Students learn how articles, registered items, enterprise names, legal representatives, business scope, and public records frame later disputes.

## Key Legal Issues

- Promoter liability and pre-incorporation transactions.
- Mandatory and optional content of articles of association.
- Company-specific registration, enterprise-name declaration, amendment procedures, and reliance on public records.
- Business scope, corporate capacity, legal representative authority, and ultra vires transactions.
- Unauthorized company guarantees and the counterparty's reasonable-review duty.
- Foreign-invested enterprise organization after the Foreign Investment Law.
- Comparative approaches to articles, bylaws, incorporation filings, and ultra vires in Hong Kong, Singapore, the UK, and Delaware.

## Hypotheticals

- A promoter signs a lease before registration and later tells the landlord to sue the new company only.
- Articles restrict transfers among employee-shareholders, but one employee leaves and challenges the repurchase clause.
- A company registers a broad business scope, but its articles require board approval before any external guarantee; the legal representative signs a guarantee without a board or shareholder resolution.
- A foreign investor wants a China subsidiary and asks which steps are corporate-law steps and which are investment-control steps.
- A lender checks the business license and company seal but does not ask for the articles or approval resolution before relying on the legal representative's signature.

## Legislation

Use the Company Law for formation, articles, legal representatives, business scope, registered capital, and company guarantees. Use the Civil Code and the Contract Book Interpretation for representative authority, contract attribution, ratification, and apparent-authority analysis.

Use the market-entity registration regulation, implementing rules, company-registration measures, and enterprise-name rules for filings, registered particulars, amendment registration, name declaration, and public-record effects. Use the Foreign Investment Law, its implementing regulation, information reporting measures, and the current negative list when formation involves foreign investors.

The comparative statutes help students separate three questions that often blur together in practice: what must be in the constitutional document, what must be filed with the public register, and whether a third party can rely on a company's apparent capacity or representative authority.

## Cases

Guiding Case No. 96 tests how far articles and initial shareholder consent can regulate exit, transfer, and repurchase arrangements in a restructured company. Guiding Case No. 10 adds the boundary between court review of resolution legality and judicial reluctance to second-guess business judgment.

The legal-representative registration cases show how articles and internal resolutions interact with the public register when the company has removed a nominee or former representative. The Merchants Bank/Zebon Gazette case gives students a concrete ultra vires guarantee problem before they read the Jiu Min Minutes and the Contract Book Interpretation.

## Readings

The practice readings give a quick map of the revised Company Law, the company-registration measures, articles-of-association drafting, and the foreign-investment reporting system. The capital-registration readings help students see why formation documents now matter for later creditor and contribution disputes.

For ultra vires, pair the Chinese scholarship on legal representative overreach and creditor review duties with Wolff's comparative account of ultra vires in Greater China. That pairing keeps the discussion from collapsing business scope, corporate capacity, and internal authority into a single label.
