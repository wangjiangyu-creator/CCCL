## Introduction

This unit moves from shareholder ownership to shareholder remedies. Students identify which rights can be exercised directly, which belong to the company, and when a minority shareholder can force judicial intervention.

## Key Legal Issues

- Shareholder status, registers, registration records, nominee arrangements, and recognition disputes.
- Information and inspection rights, including accounting vouchers and subsidiary-level access.
- Voting, proposals, shareholder meetings, resolution validity, revocation, and non-establishment.
- Profit distribution, repurchase rights, share reductions, and listed-company dividend discipline.
- Equity transfer, pre-emption, transfer restrictions, shareholder agreements, and articles.
- Derivative litigation, representative litigation, shareholder oppression, exit, and dissolution.

## Hypotheticals

- A nominee shareholder and actual investor dispute who can exercise information and voting rights after registration and shareholder-register entries diverge.
- A minority shareholder asks to inspect accounts and vouchers after suspecting related-party diversion through a wholly owned subsidiary.
- Majority shareholders refuse dividends for several profitable years while paying insiders through service contracts.
- A controller amends articles, shortens a contribution period, or blocks transfer to force a minority exit.
- A listed-company major shareholder seeks to reduce its holdings while dividend, disclosure, and lock-up restrictions apply.
- A 50 percent shareholder seeks dissolution or a buyout after all meetings fail.

## Legislation

Use the Company Law with the Civil Code, registration regulations, Interpretations II, III, IV, and V, the temporal-effect provisions, and the latest SPC draft interpretation for status recognition, information rights, resolution challenges, contribution-linked disputes, distributions, derivative claims, and dissolution. CSRC, stock-exchange, NEEQ, SASAC, and foreign-investment instruments add public-company, state-owned-shareholder, and market-transfer layers, while UK and Hong Kong statutes supply comparative reference points.

## Cases

Guiding Case No. 8 addresses dissolution as a deadlock remedy. Guiding Case No. 96 tests article-based restrictions. The CICC information-rights case, Gazette resolution cases, contribution-period cases, malicious-transfer cases, and CSRC investor-protection cases show how status, inspection, voting, transfer, distribution, and derivative remedies work in concrete disputes. Foss v. Harbottle and the common-law oppression cases explain why derivative actions and buyouts are exceptional but important minority remedies.

## Readings

The SPC press materials are useful for seeing the recurring practical disputes behind Interpretation IV. The shareholder-register, shareholder-agreement, inspection-right, derivative-action, and controller-duty readings sharpen the doctrinal problems, while the practice notes translate the 2023 Company Law into dispute strategy on information rights, buyout exits, share reductions, and minority protection. The OECD and fiduciary-duty readings connect minority protection to governance design and enforcement.
