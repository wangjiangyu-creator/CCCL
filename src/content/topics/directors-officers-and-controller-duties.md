## Introduction

This unit asks what it means to manage another person's corporate property after the 2023 Company Law revision. Students compare Chinese duties of loyalty and diligence with comparative fiduciary-duty doctrine, while paying close attention to supervisors, controlling shareholders, actual controllers, and shadow decision-makers.

## Key Legal Issues

- Duties of loyalty and diligence owed by directors, supervisors, and senior officers.
- Company-interest harm claims involving related-party transactions, competing business, corporate opportunities, fund occupation, and false disclosure.
- Capital-call responsibility for unpaid subscribed capital and the difference between shareholder contribution liability and director diligence liability.
- Supervisor responsibility as both an internal monitoring duty and a litigation-gatekeeping role.
- Controlling-shareholder and actual-controller liability when formal board action reflects external pressure or tunneling.
- Third-party and creditor-facing liability when director or officer conduct involves intentional or grossly negligent harm outside the company.
- Listed-company overlays: independent directors, management share trading, share reductions, audit committees, self-regulatory exchange rules, and information disclosure.
- Special tension for state-invested companies, dual loyalty, and public-asset accountability.

## Hypotheticals

- A controlling shareholder directs the board to approve a related-party purchase at an inflated price.
- Directors ignore repeated compliance warnings before a regulatory fine.
- A senior officer and two supervisors form a competing company and divert a supplier contract.
- Directors do not call unpaid subscribed capital before the company becomes distressed.
- A board approves a dividend or affiliate transfer when the company can no longer pay due debts.
- A supervisor considers filing a representative action after the board refuses to sue an affiliate.
- A state-invested company director claims the state investor's instruction overrides the company's interest.

## Legislation

Use the Company Law's duty, controller, and transition provisions together with Interpretations III, IV, and V, the draft SPC interpretation, the Contract Book Interpretation, and the Jiu Min Minutes. The core statutory map is wider than Articles 180 to 192. Article 22 supplies the general anti-abuse rule for related relationships. Article 51 creates the board's capital-call duty for unpaid subscribed capital. Article 89(3) connects controller oppression to a statutory exit remedy. Articles 180 to 192 then provide the central duty architecture: loyalty and diligence, related-party transaction reporting, corporate opportunities, competing business, disgorgement, damages to the company, third-party liability for intentional or grossly negligent harm, and joint liability where a controller instructs directors or senior officers to harm the company or shareholders.

For judicial interpretation, use Interpretation III for capital contribution, defective contribution, nominee shareholding, and director-side capital discipline; Interpretation V for related-party transaction liability and representative-action settlement; and the Contract Book Interpretation Article 20 for ultra vires representation by legal representatives or responsible persons. These materials help students move from abstract duty language to transaction attribution, procedure, fault, causation, and remedy.

For listed companies, use the current CSRC governance code and listed-company articles guidelines rather than the older 2018 and 2022 instruments, while noting the older instruments for historical comparison when needed. Add the information disclosure measures, SSE/SZSE/BSE listing rules, NEEQ rules, independent-director reform materials, director-supervisor-senior-manager shareholding rules, shareholder share-reduction rules, SSE and SZSE standardized-operation guidelines, and NEEQ governance rules. These instruments turn the general duty provisions into operational rules on voting, disclosure, internal control, audit committees, fund occupation, guarantees, share trading, related-party transactions, and controlling-shareholder behavior. State-owned asset instruments supply the public-asset overlay.

## Cases

Chinese cases should be organized by actor and wrong. For loyalty breaches, use the Shanghai Fluid Equipment corporate-opportunity case, Shenzhen competing-business matter, and Shangu Turbine related-party transaction case to separate company opportunity, competing business, and unnecessary affiliate extraction. The Zhengzhou Siwei and Shaanxi Real Estate cases focus on supervisor action, supervisor responsibility, finance-role knowledge, and company-interest harm. Simante should now be taught through the 2025 retrial report: Article 51 matters, but director liability still requires fault, causation, and proportionate responsibility.

Modern Avenue and the short-swing disgorgement case show derivative enforcement against controlling-shareholder or major-shareholder benefits. Kangmei, Zeda Yisheng, and Saiwei show how director, officer, controller, issuer, and assisting-counterparty responsibility appears in securities-market enforcement. The legal-representative cases remain useful for separating formal title, actual control, registration abuse, and personal responsibility.

Caremark, Van Gorkom, and Disney remain comparative anchors for oversight, board process, business judgment, and good faith. Regal, Guth, Bhullar, and Kumagai-Zenecon sharpen corporate-opportunity analysis. Sinclair, Weinberger, and MFW provide controller-transaction comparators, moving from strict controller fairness review to procedural cleansing. These cases should be contrasted with Chinese statutory language rather than imported wholesale.

## Readings

The readings now combine Chinese doctrinal work, empirical studies, practice notes, and comparative fiduciary-duty accounts. Use Shi, Zhao Lei, Wang Xiangchun, Lin Yiying, Deng, Wang Zhenzhen, and Cai Lifeng to map the revised Chinese duty system: fiduciary architecture, interest-conflict transactions, company-interest harm, diligence standards, supervisor responsibility, and controller responsibility. Use Zhu Ciyun and Zhao Xudong for the controlling-shareholder problem behind Articles 22, 89(3), and 192.

Use Howson, Xiong, Xu, Lin-Lin, Wang Jiangyu, Howson-Clarke, Huang, Liu Junhai, and Zhang to test how far common-law fiduciary-duty and derivative-action ideas travel into Chinese law. Use Hines and the liquidation-duty reading to examine dual loyalty, distress, and creditor-facing responsibility. The Cheng veil-piercing article belongs primarily with Unit 7, but it is also useful here when controller abuse is routed through sister companies or common-control structures.
