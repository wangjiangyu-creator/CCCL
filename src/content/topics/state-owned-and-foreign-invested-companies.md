## Introduction

This unit asks how company law changes when ownership is politically or internationally charged. Students compare state-invested companies, restructured enterprises, foreign-invested enterprises, and companies with cross-border capital plans.

## Key Legal Issues

- State investor functions and state-owned asset supervision.
- Governance of state-invested companies under the revised Company Law.
- Foreign-invested company organization after the Foreign Investment Law.
- National treatment, negative lists, information reporting, and security review.

## Hypotheticals

- A state-owned parent instructs a subsidiary board to sell assets below market to another state affiliate.
- A foreign investor acquires shares in a domestic company in a restricted sector.
- Employee-shareholders in a restructured enterprise challenge a charter repurchase clause.

## Legislation

Use the Company Law's state-invested company provisions with the State-Owned Assets Law, SASAC/state-owned asset supervision rules, and state-asset transaction measures. For foreign-invested companies, use the Foreign Investment Law, its implementing regulation, information reporting measures, current negative list, security-review measures, and overseas listing rules.

## Cases

Guiding Case No. 96 shows how restructuring history, employee status, and articles can shape shareholder identity and exit.

## Readings

Hines and the OECD listed-company report help students connect ownership identity to governance practice. Add the OECD SOE guidelines, the China governance country note, Milhaupt and Zheng on state capitalism, and the official foreign-investment Q&As to separate formal ownership, state influence, and investment-control rules.
