## Introduction

This unit asks what the Company Law counts as a company and why that classification changes the allocation of risk. Students compare limited liability companies, companies limited by shares, branches, partnerships, and informal business arrangements.

## Key Legal Issues

- Separate legal personality and independent corporate property.
- Limited shareholder liability and the conditions under which it becomes unstable.
- The relationship between Chinese statutory language and common-law corporate personality.
- Why business form matters for creditors, employees, managers, and investors.

## Hypotheticals

- A founder signs supply contracts before registration and later argues the company alone is liable.
- Three affiliated entities use one bank account, one sales team, and interchangeable invoices.
- A Hong Kong holding company operates a Mainland subsidiary and asks whether the parent is insulated from operating liabilities.

## Legislation

Begin with the Company Law's general provisions on company status, shareholder liability, branches, subsidiaries, and legal representatives. Use the Civil Code for legal-person capacity and agency, the market-entity registration materials for public filing, and the partnership and sole proprietorship statutes to show what Chinese law treats as non-company business organization. Compare Hong Kong and UK company legislation for the common-law baseline.

## Cases

Salomon gives the common-law baseline for separate personality. Guiding Case No. 15 shows how Chinese courts can respond when affiliated companies blur identity and assets.

## Readings

Use the G20/OECD Principles for the governance baseline. The veil-piercing readings then test the limits of separate personality in China and comparatively, while the practice notes give a quick map of how the 2023 revision changes the company/non-company boundary in real transactions.
